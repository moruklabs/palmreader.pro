What the mounts actually mean

April 24, 2026 · 4 min read

Beginner palmistry books usually skip the mounts because they are harder to photograph and harder to describe. That is a mistake. The lines tell you the pattern; the mounts tell you the volume. A faint line crossing a developed mount reads louder than a deep line across a flat one.

The seven classical mounts

Each mount is named after a planet and sits under a finger or along the palm edge. The tradition is Greco-Roman and predates modern astrology — think of the names as labels for temperaments, not horoscopes.

  • Jupiter (under the index) — ambition, leadership, self-regard.
  • Saturn (under the middle) — discipline, introspection, patience.
  • Apollo (under the ring) — creativity, visibility, charm.
  • Mercury (under the pinky) — communication, commerce, wit.
  • Venus (base of thumb) — vitality, warmth, desire.
  • Luna (opposite Venus, pinky side) — imagination, inwardness, dreams.
  • Mars (two areas, upper and lower) — courage, endurance, willingness to fight.
A line is a sentence. A mount is the tone of voice it is said in.

Developed, flat, overdeveloped

A developed mount is springy, rounded, and slightly raised. Read it as that quality being available and in working order. A flat mount is the quality being dormant — not absent, just not active. An overdeveloped mount (hard, bulging) usually reads as the quality overplayed: too much Jupiter becomes arrogance, too much Venus becomes indulgence, too much Luna becomes avoidance. The mounts give you the correction terms, not just the traits.

Cross-reads with lines

A heart line that runs into a full Jupiter mount reads as love tied to ambition and respect. The same heart line ending on a full Luna mount reads as love pulled toward imagination and escape. Same line — totally different relationship. That is why the mounts matter: they are the modifier on every reading nearby.

How the app uses mounts

Palm Reader segments the mount regions from a single hand photo and estimates which are prominent. That estimate is fed into the reading as context for the line interpretations, so the write-up reflects the shape of the whole palm, not just the lines. If the photo is too flat to judge, the app says so rather than guessing.
